What can I use instead of Aleppo pepper?

Posted by Martina Birk on Thursday, September 5, 2024

Answer

Hot paprika, cayenne pepper, or crushed red pepper flakes can be substituted for the paprika. Ancho chile powder would be the most similar substitution, and it could even be blended with a pinch of salt.

Is there a substitute for Aleppo pepper, in light of this?

If you are unable to locate Aleppo pepper, a blend of Hungarian sweet paprika and cayenne pepper can be used in place of it. Make sure not to overdo it on the cayenne pepper—a little sprinkle should be sufficient. Alternatively, crushed red pepper can be used as a substitute if you don’t have either of these spices on hand.

One can also wonder if Aleppo pepper and Pul Biber are the same thing.

Halaby (or Halab pepper) is another name for the Aleppo pepper, which is also known as the Aleppo chilli in some parts of the world. In Turkey, the crushed, dried flakes are known as pul biber, or in English language Turkish recipe books as ‘Turkish pepper flakes.’ As a result, Aleppo pepper is not pepper (go to Full Briefing on Pepper to find out more on pepper).

In a similar vein, what does Aleppo pepper flavour like?

The Aleppo pepper has a moderate heat rating of approximately 10,000 on the Scoville scale, and it has a fruity flavour with faint cumin-like undertones. Its flavour is comparable to that of the ancho chile, but it is oilier and slightly saltier due to the fact that salt is frequently utilised during the drying process.

What is the best way to prepare Aleppo peppers?

It can be used in a variety of ways, including marinating roast chicken in olive oil, rubbing it on roasted vegetables, mixing it into salad dressing, and even rimming a cocktail glass. Really, you can use this spice in anything that calls for a layer of slow-building heat to be present.
